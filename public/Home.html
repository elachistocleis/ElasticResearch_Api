The Fight for Open Access information

Open science is one of the latest science movements to provide free access to science information. This science trend follows a set of principles and a range of practices that facilitate online information distribution.

This movement is a consequence of the peculiar and predatory nature of science publishing, especially in the US. Basically, for unethical business practices (2):

• A scientist does research, often with taxpayer money.

• A publishing company sets up hundreds of scientific journals.

• The scientist writes a paper.

• The scientist pays the publisher to look up papers in their journals.

• Other scientists pay the publisher to read the paper.

• Students pay the publisher to read the paper.

• Online users pay the publisher to read the paper.

• The publisher keeps all the money. The people who write the papers get no royalties. Often they can’t even read or reference their own papers online for free—they have to pay the publisher to print them and pay the publisher to access them.

One of the most controversial facts about this problem is that the scientists get no royalties even though the publisher charges to access them. One infamous example of this business model is Elsevier, a huge science publisher that is characterized by this unethical practice (3). Some proposals to solve this problem include legal alternative as google books that offer partial access to books and the option to buy digital versions at a low cost. Other alternatives as Genesis library and Sci-Hub offer access to papers and books violating the copyrights of the authors and the publishers.
